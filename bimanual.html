TL;DR — I led a 20-student team at ETH Robotics Club to finetune ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) on a custom bimanual robot setup, getting it to autonomously fold towels — a classic long-horizon, deformable-object manipulation benchmark. We built the hardware from scratch, collected teleoperated demonstration data, and iterated through multiple rounds of training and deployment.

The Project

Folding a towel sounds trivial — until you ask a robot to do it. Towels are deformable, high-dimensional, and visually ambiguous; a bimanual system has to coordinate two arms through a long sequence of grasps, lifts, and re-grasps, with every step depending on the outcome of the previous one. It's a textbook long-horizon manipulation task, and a widely used benchmark in robot learning research.

Over the course of the 2025 semester, I led a team of 20 students from the ETH Robotics Club to replicate and extend this result on our own hardware. The goal was to take a state-of-the-art imitation learning algorithm — ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers), originally introduced as part of the ALOHA project — and get it running autonomously on a bimanual setup we built ourselves.

The Hardware Setup

We assembled a bimanual manipulation station around two YAM arms with parallel-jaw grippers, mounted on a rigid frame with multiple camera viewpoints for visual servoing and data collection. A teleoperation rig let a human operator move both arms simultaneously via a leader-follower configuration — this is the pipeline we used to collect demonstration data for training.

Left: the bimanual setup during training runs. Right: part of the team behind the project.

The Approach — ACT

ACT (Action Chunking with Transformers) is an imitation learning policy that predicts chunks of future actions from visual observations rather than single-step actions. The chunking trick helps tremendously with compounding error over long horizons, which is exactly the failure mode that breaks naive behaviour cloning on tasks like folding.

  • Inputs: multi-view RGB observations + current joint states of both arms.
  • Outputs: a chunk of future joint-space actions for both arms simultaneously.
  • Architecture: a transformer encoder-decoder trained with a conditional-VAE objective for smoother action distributions.
  • Training data: teleoperated demonstrations collected on our own hardware.

Data Collection & Training

The bulk of the semester went into the unglamorous but critical loop of collect data → train → deploy → watch it fail → collect more data. We recorded hundreds of teleoperated demonstrations of towel folding, covering variations in initial towel configuration, lighting, and arm starting poses. Each demonstration captured synchronised joint states, gripper commands, and multiple camera streams.

Training was done on finetuned ACT checkpoints, progressively increasing dataset size and tuning the chunk size, learning rate schedule, and observation encoders to our specific setup.

What I Learned

  • Hardware iteration is the real work. The algorithm matters, but so does mount rigidity, camera placement, latency in the teleop pipeline, and gripper repeatability. We rebuilt parts of the setup three times.
  • Data quality > data quantity. A small set of clean, consistent demonstrations outperformed a larger noisy one.
  • Leading a 20-person volunteer team across hardware, data, and ML sub-teams — coordinating, unblocking people, and keeping the critical path moving — was the hardest and most rewarding part.
